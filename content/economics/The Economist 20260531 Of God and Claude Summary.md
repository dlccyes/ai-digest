## The deeper target

The article's sharper point is that Leo is not only asking governments to write better AI rules. He is challenging two ideas that have become influential in parts of the technology world: transhumanism and posthumanism.

Transhumanism imagines the enhancement of people through tools such as body engineering, devices and algorithms. Posthumanism goes further, imagining a merger of humans, machines and the surrounding environment that could eventually carry humanity into a new evolutionary stage. The Economist connects this to the tech world's language of the Singularity, the hypothesised moment when technological progress produces forms of intelligence and capability far beyond ordinary human limits.

Leo's objection is not simply that these ideas sound strange. It is that they treat humanity as a defective thing to be optimised. The encyclical argues that this desire to perfect or surpass the human species can become morally dangerous, because it encourages people to sort lives into more useful and less useful categories. The article highlights the pope's comparison with eugenics, the 20th-century ideology that promised biological improvement and helped justify monstrous politics.

Against that, Leo defends human limitation. Frailty, failure, scars and dependence are not bugs in the human condition. They are part of what makes moral and spiritual life possible. In the article's reading, the encyclical is insisting that dignity cannot depend on upgradeability. A person does not become more worthy by becoming more efficient, less vulnerable or more machine-like.

## A rival eschatology

The Economist treats the encyclical as the first shot in a philosophical turf war. Some AI optimism has the shape of a secular religion: a coming transformation, a new intelligence, a path to god-like capacities. That story resembles Christian expectation while replacing salvation with technical acceleration. The pope's challenge is therefore not only legal or practical. It is theological. He rejects the idea that artificial general intelligence should be treated as a messianic event.

That makes the politics awkward. Some prominent AI enthusiasts on the American right, including influential investors, are also vocal Christians. The debate is not a simple contest between religion and technology, or between Europe and America. It is a dispute inside a wider culture that wants both spiritual language and technological transcendence.

The article is careful to say that Leo does not reject technology itself. He acknowledges that technological development has improved human life. The argument is about limits, purpose and power. Technology is acceptable when it serves human flourishing. It becomes dangerous when it turns human beings into raw material for optimisation or treats their replacement as progress.
