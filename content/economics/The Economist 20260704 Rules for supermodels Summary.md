## Why permanent containment will fail

Licensing frontier models is unlikely to disappear, because their strongest capabilities create genuine security risks. Yet an indefinite block would be both unworkable and harmful. Chinese labs are only months behind the American frontier, and many of their models are open-weight, allowing users to run and modify them. Chip shortages and tighter controls on model distillation may slow Chinese firms, but probably only temporarily. Even a broad American ban would leave them a vast domestic market.

Restrictions would also hurt American researchers and companies that use inexpensive, adaptable Chinese models. More broadly, the same intelligence that makes advanced systems dangerous also makes them valuable. If regulators suppress several generations of progress and then release them at once, society loses the chance to adapt gradually. A small group with privileged access gains unusual power, while everyone else faces a sudden and potentially chaotic leap in capability.

## Separate political choices from technical judgments

The article supports an emerging practice: evaluate a frontier model before release, then introduce it gradually through trusted institutions. But that process should be formal rather than negotiated case by case between politicians and AI companies.

Elected leaders should decide the acceptable level of risk because that is a public choice. They should not personally manage model tests or bargain over individual releases. Technical evaluation should instead sit with a specialist structure that combines expertise from government institutes, AI labs, independent researchers and allied regulators. Industries such as finance and electricity offer a useful pattern: expert bodies conduct oversight within goals and limits set by government.

International co-operation would make this system stronger, but America may resist sharing control over technology that gives it immense strategic leverage. The article therefore advises other countries to build bargaining power and resilience of their own. They need credible AI sectors, workable regulations and contingency plans that let businesses move to non-American models running in non-American data centres if export controls suddenly cut access.

The conclusion is not that allies should trust China instead. Dependence on America remains far safer. But relying on any single government for access to frontier intelligence is now a strategic vulnerability. Sensible AI regulation must reduce catastrophic risk without turning model access into an arbitrary political weapon.
