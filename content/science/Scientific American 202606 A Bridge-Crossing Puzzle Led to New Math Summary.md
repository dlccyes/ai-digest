The puzzle came from the 18th-century Prussian city of Konigsberg, where residents wondered whether a person could walk through the city and cross each of its seven bridges exactly once. The answer was no, but the more important result was the method Leonhard Euler used to prove it. In solving a recreational question that he first considered too slight for serious mathematics, Euler helped create graph theory and pointed toward topology.

## The Puzzle Hidden in a Walk

The bridge problem looked geographic. It involved islands, riverbanks and routes through a real city. A map seemed like the natural object to study. Murtagh explains that Euler's decisive move was to notice which parts of the map mattered and which did not. The lengths of the bridges did not matter. Neither did the shapes of the islands, the angles of the crossings or the actual scale of the city. The only important facts were the land regions and the bridges connecting them.

That act of stripping away details is abstraction. Once the city is reduced to landmasses and connections, it becomes a graph: vertices stand for pieces of land, and edges stand for bridges. This is not a graph in the sense of a plotted curve or a bar chart. It is a network. The original walking problem then becomes a general question: given any network of vertices and edges, is there a path that uses every edge exactly once?

Euler found a strikingly simple obstruction. At each vertex, count how many edges touch it. If a walker enters a vertex in the middle of the route, the walker must also leave it by a different unused edge. Middle vertices therefore need their edges to pair off: one for entering and one for leaving. Only the starting and ending vertices can have an odd number of incident edges, because the start does not need an entering edge and the end does not need an exiting edge. A route that crosses every edge exactly once is possible only when all vertices have even degree or exactly two vertices have odd degree.

Konigsberg failed that test. All four land regions had an odd number of bridges touching them, so no amount of clever route planning could produce the desired walk. The townspeople had not missed a trick; the network itself made the walk impossible.

## A New Kind of Mathematical Object

Murtagh emphasizes that the bridge problem mattered because it did not fit neatly into the mathematical categories Euler inherited. It was not solved by measuring distance, area or angle. It was not an algebraic calculation. It required attention to connection itself. That shift helped launch graph theory, a field that now underlies computer science, logistics, social network analysis, biology, the web, neural networks and many other systems built from pairwise relationships.

The article also uses the bridge puzzle to show how close-looking problems can belong to very different mathematical worlds. A path that crosses every edge once is now called an Eulerian path, and it has a simple test. But a path that visits every vertex once, known as a Hamiltonian path, is far harder. It sounds like a small variation, yet mathematicians and computer scientists do not know a general efficient method for solving it. The contrast is a useful warning: changing what must be visited can transform a problem from transparent to computationally stubborn.

Euler's abstraction also helped prepare the ground for topology. Topology studies properties that survive continuous deformation, such as stretching or squeezing, while ignoring details such as precise lengths and angles. In the bridge problem, Euler similarly ignored metric features and kept only the pattern of connections. The result was a form of geometry without measurement, one concerned less with exact shape than with how pieces relate.

## Why the Old Puzzle Still Feels Modern

The lasting point of the article is not merely that Euler solved a charming puzzle. It is that the solution shows how mathematical progress often begins when someone invents the right representation. The map of Konigsberg was too detailed for the question being asked. The graph was not a simplified picture in a casual sense; it was the problem's true skeleton.

That move recurs throughout science and mathematics. Researchers turn messy physical systems into equations, organisms into family trees, cities into transport networks, molecules into interaction graphs and websites into linked nodes. Each abstraction gives up some information to preserve what matters for a particular question. When the abstraction is well chosen, a problem that once looked like a tangle can suddenly admit a clean theorem.

Murtagh's closing insight is that obvious abstractions often look obvious only after someone has made them. The seven bridges of Konigsberg taught mathematicians to see networks as objects in their own right. From a failed walk through a city came a language for reasoning about routes, connections and deformations across modern mathematics and computing. The puzzle's answer was negative, but the mathematics it opened was expansive.
