Memory is fallible, but that does not mean it is easy to rewrite wholesale. People forget details, confuse sources and absorb misleading suggestions. The more alarming claim is that an experimenter or therapist can readily implant a vivid memory of an entire event that never occurred. Psychologists Bernice Andrews and Chris R. Brewin argue that the evidence for this stronger claim is much weaker than it is often made to seem.

That distinction matters beyond the laboratory. Claims about implanted memories have been used in court to cast doubt on testimony, including allegations of abuse. If ordinary suggestion can manufacture whole experiences, then even sincere witnesses may seem fundamentally unreliable. But if the research mostly demonstrates uncertainty, fragmentary recall or confusion between similar events, its legal meaning is far narrower.

## Revisiting a Famous Experiment

The article begins with the 1995 "lost in the mall" experiment by Elizabeth Loftus and Jacqueline Pickrell. Twenty-four participants were told that a parent or older sibling had reported a childhood incident in which they became lost in a shopping mall and were rescued by an older woman. The relatives had confirmed that the event never happened. After two sessions encouraging participants to remember it, the researchers classified one quarter of them as having formed a full or partial false memory.

The experiment became an influential demonstration that suggestion could create recollections of entire fictional events. Similar studies later reported memories of spilling a punch bowl at a wedding, riding in a hot-air balloon or putting slime in a teacher's desk. Yet Andrews and Brewin saw two unresolved problems: whether participants themselves believed they truly remembered the suggested event, and whether the details they supplied were actually false rather than drawn from real, related experiences.

A 2023 replication created an unusual opportunity to test those questions. Gillian Murphy and her colleagues followed the original procedure with 123 participants and made their data and interview transcripts openly available. Their initial analysis classified 35 percent, or 43 people, as having a false memory. But when participants were directly asked whether they remembered the invented event, only 14 percent said that they did. The gap suggested that experimenters' labels and participants' own experience were not measuring the same thing.

## What the Participants Actually Remembered

Andrews and Brewin broke the invented story into six defining elements: the participant was about five years old, was lost for an extended time, cried, was found by an older woman, was reunited with family and was lost in the specific shopping location suggested by the researchers.

None of the participants recalled all six elements. Those classified as having a full false memory supplied fewer than three on average, while those assigned a partial false memory supplied about one. Even more tellingly, 20 percent of the "full" group and 60 percent of the "partial" group did not explicitly remember the central event of being lost.

Half of the people labeled as having false memories had actually been lost before or had lived through an analogous experience. Their comments often separated the real episode from the researchers' suggestion. Others reasoned through possibilities, compared the story with genuine memories or expressed substantial doubt. Calling all of this a false memory flattened meaningful differences among remembering, believing, speculating and trying to cooperate with an experiment.

After reviewing both the transcripts and the story elements, Andrews and Brewin concluded that only five participants could reasonably be said to have formed a false memory, rather than the 43 in the original classification. Their reanalysis does not show that memory is perfectly accurate or immune to misinformation. Earlier work clearly demonstrates that leading questions can alter remembered details. It instead challenges the larger leap from distorted details to confident recollection of a wholly invented event.

## A More Careful Lesson about Memory

The article's strongest point is methodological. A scientific category is only as sound as the evidence used to assign it. Open transcripts allowed independent researchers to see that a broad label such as "false memory" sometimes covered hesitant guesses, fragments of real experiences and explicit rejection of the suggested story. Without that scrutiny, a striking percentage could travel far beyond the experiment while concealing what participants had actually said.

The legal takeaway is deliberately balanced. Memory should be examined critically, especially when testimony carries grave consequences, but the mere possibility of error does not justify treating a person's account as disposable. The evidence reviewed here suggests that entirely fictional autobiographical memories are difficult to implant and usually less complete and less confidently held than headline claims imply. Human memory is not a recording, but neither is it soft clay that others can effortlessly mold into any story.
