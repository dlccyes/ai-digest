    Two Ways to Have a Good Life

    A screaming baby at two in the morning can make a parent miserable without making parenthood feel meaningless. That tension is the “parenting paradox”: compared with adults without children, parents often report more stress, depression and low moods in daily life, yet they can also describe their lives as more satisfying overall.

    In The Neuroscience of the “Parenting Paradox”, emotion neuroscientist Anthony Vaccaro argues that the contradiction becomes easier to understand once well-being is separated into different layers. Immediate feelings register whether an experience is pleasant or painful. A broader sense of meaning asks whether life feels coherent, purposeful and connected to long-term goals. Those judgments can move independently. Sleeplessness and frustration may dominate the moment while parenthood still fits a person’s larger understanding of who they are.

    Psychologists measure meaning through people’s reports of whether their lives make sense and have an overarching purpose. This is not just a philosophical extra: a stronger sense of meaning is associated with better mental health and greater resilience during events such as serious illness, war and a pandemic. Vaccaro’s central proposal is that the brain’s ability to place mixed emotions inside a coherent self-narrative helps explain how hardship and fulfillment can coexist.

    Following First-Time Fathers through a Major Change

    Vaccaro and his colleagues followed 88 first-time fathers from their partners’ pregnancies until about six months after the births. The researchers expected most participants to report that life had become more meaningful. Instead the group divided roughly in half: some fathers’ sense of meaning increased, while others’ decreased. Parenthood did not produce a universal psychological benefit.

    Thirty-five of the fathers underwent functional magnetic resonance imaging both before and after the birth. The team examined functional connectivity—how closely activity in different brain regions rises and falls together—and compared its changes with the fathers’ reports about meaning and their positive or negative feelings toward parenting.

    Different patterns accompanied different parts of the experience. More positive parenting feelings were associated with connectivity changes in regions involved in self-control and empathy, including the middle frontal gyrus and supramarginal gyrus. More negative feelings accompanied changes in the sensory cortex and cerebellum, which the researchers suggest might reflect heightened emotional sensitivity to sensory signals such as a baby’s cry.

    Fathers whose sense of meaning held steady or increased showed more connectivity in the insular cortex and temporal pole. These regions help combine emotion and sensation with identity and abstract thought. That association supports the idea that integrative brain processes allow a difficult event to become part of a meaningful life story rather than letting its immediate discomfort define well-being as a whole.

    The evidence is suggestive, not definitive. The imaging sample was small, the results show associations rather than proof that connectivity caused the reported feelings, and a study of first-time fathers cannot by itself describe the experiences of mothers, other parents or families in different circumstances. The value of the work lies in identifying a plausible distinction for larger and more diverse studies to test.

    Meaning Depends on the Goal, Not the Role

    The article connects this brain research to a long-term analysis of German adults surveyed between 1984 and 2021. Among middle-aged adults, researchers found no average difference in well-being between people with children and those without, although parents’ experiences varied more. For younger adults, the important factor was not parenthood itself but the importance they assigned to becoming a parent.

    Childless young adults whose desire for children remained highly important tended to become less satisfied as they aged. Most child-free adults, however, reduced the importance of that goal over time, and their happiness eventually looked no different from that of adults with children. A meaningful life did not require one predetermined outcome; people could revise their goals and the stories they told about themselves.

    This perspective does not make stress imaginary or guarantee that reframing a life will solve serious problems. It does show why asking whether children make people happier is too blunt. Momentary happiness, long-term satisfaction and a sense of purpose are related but distinct experiences. Parenthood can raise one while lowering another, and choosing not to become a parent can be equally compatible with flourishing. What matters most is whether lived experience can be integrated with a person’s evolving goals and identity.
